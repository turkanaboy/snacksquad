# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Carnegie Higher Education employees using Snack Squad during the workday, primarily on desktop with a mobile-friendly companion experience. They want to log snacks quickly, see what coworkers are enjoying, vote, compete in lightweight contests, and build shared office culture without turning the activity into administrative work.

## Product Purpose

Snack Squad is a playful internal snack logger and competition hub. It combines one-tap personal logging, a shared activity board, canonical snack rankings, weekly brackets, earned badges, and—after the core community is active—a monthly fantasy league. Success means repeat participation: more than five daily active users, average usage of three logs per person per week, a growing user base, and at least one fully participated bracket.

## Brand Personality

Playful, competitive, polished. The experience should feel welcoming and quick during a work break, with the clarity and trust of a best-in-class product interface and selective collectible snack-culture moments that make participation memorable.

## Anti-references

Do not make Snack Squad resemble a generic SaaS analytics dashboard, a childish candy-store website, a noisy sports-betting interface, or a template-driven AI product made from interchangeable cards, gradients, glass panels, and decorative metrics. Competition should feel spirited rather than aggressive, and food imagery should support recognition rather than overwhelm the task.

## Design Principles

1. Make the next useful action obvious and fast.
2. Let snack identity and coworker activity create the personality; keep the product mechanics familiar.
3. Make competition legible, fair, and celebratory without adding pressure.
4. Reward repeat participation with visible progress and collectible moments.
5. Prefer a cohesive, ownable system over screen-by-screen novelty.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA. Support complete keyboard operation, visible focus, adequate text and control contrast, semantic structure, accessible names and status announcements, touch-friendly targets, reduced-motion preferences, and states that never rely on color alone.
